Wardrobe Gaps — Design Approaches

Same editorial DNA as the Travel Edit. Which direction?

A

The Stylist's Pick — Recommended

Hero gap as a full-page editorial moment. Strip all card wrappers. The #1 gap fills the screen like a magazine product feature — big item name in Playfair italic, pairing garment thumbnails, "+N outfits" as a bold accent number, reason text as stylist commentary. Secondary gaps become a clean vertical list (not horizontal scroll cards) with dividers, each expandable. State views (locked, ready, loading) get the same cardless treatment with border-t dividers. The PageHeader eyebrow + title stays as the anchor.

B

The Shopping Edit

Results as a curated shopping list. Strip cards from states but keep a refined card treatment on results only — each gap becomes a "product card" like a luxury e-commerce listing. Hero gap is larger with the accent gradient. Secondary gaps are a 2-column grid of product cards. The "Find this" button becomes the primary CTA on each card. More shopping-forward, less editorial.

C

The Wardrobe Report

Full cardless with a document feel. Like receiving a printed report from your stylist. All gaps in a single vertical flow with numbered sections (1. Your next best purchase, 2. Fill your neutral gap, etc.). No cards, no horizontal scroll. Each section has the item name, reason, pairing thumbnails, and action button — separated by elegant dividers. Most editorial but least "browseable."
